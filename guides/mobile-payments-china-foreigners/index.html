Mobile Payments in China for Foreigners: Alipay, WeChat Pay & Foreign Cards

Most daily payments in Shanghai happen through QR codes on Alipay or WeChat Pay — and both apps can work with many overseas bank cards. Here is how to get set up, what fees and limits to expect, and what backups to keep ready.

Why this matters

Shanghai runs on QR codes

Cash is still useful as a backup, but Shanghai now runs mainly on QR-code payments. In daily life, mobile payment is the default for restaurants, convenience stores, taxis, cafés, malls, supermarkets, metro travel, tourist attractions, hotels, and many small shops. You do not need a Chinese bank account for everyday merchant payments, but your experience will usually be smoother if your account is verified and your card details match your passport name.

Alipay: usually the easiest starting point

Alipay is usually the simplest first app for foreign visitors and new arrivals. You can link many overseas cards, verify your identity with your passport, and use it for shops, restaurants, taxis, public transport, hotels, and many travel services. Alipay+ publishes an overview of how international visitors can pay in the Chinese mainland with overseas cards and partner wallets.

Alipay is best for:

  • First-time visitors and tourists
  • New arrivals setting up their first payment method
  • Metro and taxi payments
  • Translation and travel tools
  • Paying at most everyday merchants

Foreign card support can include major international card networks. American Express support has also expanded — Amex joined Alipay's digital payment system in 2025 — making Alipay more useful for some eligible international cardholders.

WeChat Pay: just as important for daily life

WeChat Pay matters because WeChat is used for messaging, groups, schools, landlords, communities, restaurants, businesses, and many local services. Once you live in Shanghai, a lot of daily life happens inside WeChat.

WeChat Pay is best for:

  • People already using WeChat
  • Local services and mini-programs
  • Restaurants and shops
  • Schools, landlords, and community payments
  • Day-to-day life after arrival

WeChat Pay can work with several overseas cards, but some features are limited when using an international card. Foreign cards are usually fine for merchant payments, but may not support red packets, person-to-person transfers, receiving money from friends, or some financial services.

The basics

How mobile payments work in China

  • Link Link an overseas bank card

    Both Alipay and WeChat Pay allow many foreigners to link eligible overseas cards. Commonly supported networks may include Visa, Mastercard, JCB, Diners Club, Discover, and American Express in some cases. Support varies by app, card issuer, country, and verification status — if one card fails, try another card from a different bank or network.
  • Verify Complete identity verification

    For smoother use, complete real-name verification with your passport. This can help reduce payment interruptions and may raise your available transaction limits. Make sure the name on your payment account, passport, and bank card is consistent — mismatches can cause card-linking or payment failures.
  • Pay Scan or show your QR code

    There are two common ways to pay: you scan the merchant's QR code, or the merchant scans your payment code. Both are widely used in Shanghai. For small shops, cafés, taxis, and restaurants, QR-code payment is often the easiest method.

Shanghai's government publishes an official English guide to payment and banking services for foreigners, which is a useful reference if you want to see the city's own instructions.

Money matters

Current limits and fees for foreign cards

Payment limits and fees can change, so always check the final payment screen inside Alipay or WeChat Pay. As a general guide for overseas visitors using mobile payments in China:

  • The single transaction limit has been raised to around USD 5,000, and the annual cumulative cap to around USD 50,000, following a 2024 government update for overseas visitors.
  • Some small transactions under RMB 200 may have no added platform fee.
  • A 3% fee may apply to single transactions above RMB 200.
  • Your card issuer may also charge a foreign transaction fee.
  • Refunds may return platform fees proportionally, depending on the payment provider's rules.

For daily life, this is usually enough for meals, taxis, metro rides, groceries, cafés, shops, and tourist attractions. For large payments — hotel deposits, tuition, rent, or medical bills — confirm payment options in advance.

Set expectations

What foreign cards may not support

Using an overseas card in Alipay or WeChat Pay does not always give you the same features as a local Chinese wallet. Foreign cards may not support:

  • Red packets
  • Person-to-person transfers
  • Receiving money from friends
  • Some mini-program payments
  • Some subscription or automatic deduction services
  • Wealth management or insurance products
  • Certain high-value transactions
  • Some merchant-specific QR codes

This is why it is better to treat foreign-card mobile payments as a practical spending tool, not a full replacement for a local Chinese bank account.

Practical tips

What to know before your first payment

  • 01 Set up Alipay before you need it

    Do not wait until you are standing at a checkout counter. Download Alipay, link your card, complete verification, and test a small payment as early as possible.
  • 02 Set up WeChat Pay as your second option

    Even if Alipay works well, WeChat Pay is still worth setting up. Many local services in Shanghai operate inside WeChat, and some merchants may prefer WeChat Pay.
  • 03 Use a phone number that can receive SMS codes

    You can often use an international phone number, but it must be able to receive verification codes. A Chinese SIM card or reliable eSIM can make setup and payment verification easier.
  • 04 Turn off your VPN if payments fail

    If a payment is blocked or the app behaves strangely, turn off your VPN and try again on a stable connection.
  • 05 Keep a backup card and some RMB cash

    Some hotels, airport counters, hospitals, metro service centers, and larger retailers may accept physical cards. RMB cash is useful if your phone dies, your card is blocked, or a merchant QR code fails.
  • 06 Try a small test payment first

    Before relying on Alipay or WeChat Pay for an important transaction, test with a small purchase such as a convenience store drink, metro ride, or coffee.

Getting around

Paying for transport in Shanghai

Mobile payments are useful for transport, but they are not the only option. For Shanghai Metro and public transport, foreigners may be able to use:

  • Alipay transport QR code
  • WeChat Pay transport options
  • Shanghai Metro Daduhui app
  • Suishenxing / SH MaaS app
  • Physical Shanghai Public Transportation Card
  • Shanghai Pass or tourist transport passes
  • Eligible foreign bank cards at metro service centers
  • Contactless cards on supported transport routes

The city's official Shanghai Metro payment guidance covers the options available to international visitors. If you are new to Shanghai, set up Alipay first, then prepare one backup transport method in case your QR code does not work — our Shanghai Metro guide walks through the details.

Plan B

Backup payment methods in Shanghai

Even though mobile payment is the most convenient option, foreigners should know the alternatives.

Cash

RMB cash is still useful for emergencies, small purchases, and situations where your phone or card does not work. Keep small notes rather than only large bills.

Physical bank card

Foreign cards are more useful at airports, hotels, major shopping malls, and some international retailers. They may not work at small local shops.

UnionPay

If you have access to a UnionPay card, it can be useful in China because UnionPay is widely accepted.

e-CNY / digital yuan

China's digital yuan, also called e-CNY, is another payment option for some visitors. It can be useful as a backup, but Alipay and WeChat Pay are still the most practical first choices for most foreigners.

Local Chinese bank account

If you are staying long term, opening a Chinese bank account can make payments easier. It may unlock more features, improve reliability, and make transfers more practical. Shanghai's official banking services guide explains what foreigners need.

When things go wrong

Common payment problems and fixes

  1. 01My card will not link

    Check that your card supports international online payments. Make sure the name on your card matches your registered name in the app. Try another card if available.

  2. 02I cannot receive the SMS code

    Check roaming, mobile signal, country code, and SMS permissions. If your home number is unreliable in China, use a local SIM or reliable travel eSIM.

  3. 03The payment was declined

    Try turning off your VPN, lowering the payment amount, switching from scan-to-pay to payment-code mode, or using another card.

  4. 04The merchant QR code does not work

    Ask the merchant if they can scan your payment code instead. Some personal or merchant-specific QR codes may not support foreign-card payments.

  5. 05A large payment failed

    Split the payment if the merchant allows it, use another card, pay with a physical card, or confirm whether the merchant accepts foreign-card mobile payments.

  6. 06My app asks for passport verification

    Upload your passport details clearly and make sure the information matches your account and card details.

Keeping current

Recent updates to know

China increased mobile payment limits for overseas visitors in 2024, making Alipay and WeChat Pay more useful for larger everyday transactions.

American Express cardholders may also have more options through Alipay, after Amex joined the Alipay system in 2025, depending on card eligibility.

PayPal began connecting with WeChat Pay's merchant network for U.S. PayPal users in 2026, with wider rollout expected in phases. This could become useful for some travelers, but Alipay and WeChat Pay should still be your main setup for now.

Frequently asked questions

Mobile payment questions for foreigners

Can foreigners use Alipay in China?

Yes. Many foreigners can use Alipay by linking an eligible overseas bank card and completing identity verification. See our full Alipay guide for foreigners.

Can foreigners use WeChat Pay in China?

Yes. Many foreigners can use WeChat Pay with an eligible overseas card, but some features are limited compared with a local Chinese wallet. Our WeChat Pay guide covers the setup.

Do I need a Chinese bank account?

No, not for basic merchant payments. However, a Chinese bank account can make life easier if you live in China long term.

Do I need a Chinese phone number?

Not always. An international number can work if it receives SMS verification codes. A Chinese SIM or reliable eSIM is still helpful.

Which app should I set up first?

Set up Alipay first, then WeChat Pay. Alipay is usually easier for new arrivals, while WeChat Pay becomes more important once you start using local services.

Should I still carry cash?

Yes. Carry a small amount of RMB cash for emergencies.

Can I send money to friends with a foreign card?

Usually not. Person-to-person transfers and red packets are often limited or unavailable with foreign cards.

Can I use Apple Pay or Google Pay in Shanghai?

Do not rely on Apple Pay or Google Pay as your main payment method. Alipay and WeChat Pay are far more widely used for everyday QR-code payments.
