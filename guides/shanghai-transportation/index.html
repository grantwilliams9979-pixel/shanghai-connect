Shanghai Transportation Guide: Metro, Didi, Taxis, Buses, Bikes & Ferries

Shanghai has one of the world's most extensive metro systems, reliable ride-hailing, affordable taxis, public buses, shared bikes, ferries, and multiple airport transport options. For most newcomers, the easiest starting point is simple: learn the metro first, set up mobile payment, and use Didi or taxis when public transport is inconvenient.

The basics

Most journeys need just two things

For most daily life in Shanghai, the metro and Didi cover nearly everything. The metro is fast, cheap, reliable, and connects most major residential, school, office, shopping, and tourist areas. Didi fills the gaps when you are travelling late at night, carrying luggage, going somewhere far from a station, or travelling with children. Once you are comfortable with those two options, shared bikes, buses, ferries, and airport rail links become useful additions.

One app for everything

SH MaaS / Suishenxing: the all-in-one transport app

SH MaaS, also known as Suishenxing, is one of the most useful transport apps for newcomers in Shanghai. It is designed to bring multiple city transport services into one place.

Use it for:

  • Metro, buses, and ferries
  • Airport Link Line and other urban rail services
  • Maglev information
  • Taxis and shared bikes
  • Real-time transport updates
  • First and last train information
  • Route planning
  • 1-day, 3-day, and 7-day transport passes where available

It is especially useful because it can provide one transport QR code for multiple forms of public transport, reducing the need to switch between separate apps. Best for: newcomers who want one main transport app instead of setting up every system separately. See our essential apps guide for the rest of your app setup.

Your main option

By metro

The Shanghai Metro is the easiest transport option for most newcomers. It has more than 20 lines and connects most areas where expats, students, business travellers, and visitors are likely to live, work, study, or explore.

The metro is usually the best choice for:

  • Daily commuting
  • Tourist attractions
  • Shopping areas
  • Railway stations
  • Travelling between Pudong and Puxi
  • Avoiding traffic
  • Budget-friendly travel

Why the metro is the best starting point

Shanghai traffic can be slow during rush hour, but the metro is predictable. Stations are clearly marked, trains are frequent, and most station signs include English. For new arrivals, it is usually easier to use the metro during the day and Didi or taxis at night.

Metro payment options

You can usually pay using:

  • Alipay
  • WeChat Pay
  • SH MaaS / Suishenxing
  • Shanghai Metro Daduhui app
  • Shanghai Public Transportation Card
  • Single-journey tickets
  • Eligible contactless bank cards on supported gates

Metro tips for newcomers

  • Avoid peak commuting times when possible: around 7:30–9:30 am and 5:30–7:30 pm.
  • Keep your bag in front of you during crowded times.
  • Check the final station direction before boarding.
  • Some stations are very large, so allow extra time for transfers.
  • Last trains vary by line and station, so check before travelling late at night.

When metro is not enough

By taxi and Didi

Use Didi or taxis when the metro is not convenient. This is common when you are travelling late at night, carrying luggage, going somewhere far from a station, travelling with children, or arriving during bad weather.

Didi

Didi works similarly to Uber. You enter your pickup and destination in the app, confirm the ride, and pay through the app. Didi is useful because:

  • You can enter destinations without speaking Chinese.
  • The price is shown before or during the ride.
  • You can choose vehicle types.
  • Payment is handled in the app.
  • It is easier than explaining an address to a street taxi driver.

Taxi

Shanghai taxis are still useful, especially at airports, railway stations, hotels, and major shopping areas. Official taxis should use the meter. Avoid private drivers who approach you inside airports, railway stations, or tourist areas. A real taxi should have:

  • A taxi roof light
  • A meter
  • A visible vacancy sign
  • Company markings
  • Driver information
  • A printed receipt option

Taxi tips

  • Keep your destination written in Chinese.
  • Ask the driver to use the meter if needed.
  • Do not accept rides from unofficial drivers inside terminals.
  • Keep the receipt in case you leave something behind.
  • Tipping is not expected.
  • During rain or rush hour, taxis and Didi cars can be harder to get.

First journey

Getting from the airport

Shanghai has two main airports. Pudong International Airport (PVG) is the main international airport. Hongqiao Airport (SHA) serves many domestic flights and some regional international routes, and is connected to Hongqiao Railway Station, one of the city's major high-speed rail hubs.

Pudong Airport is far from central Shanghai, so the best option depends on your arrival time, luggage, budget, and final destination.

Maglev

The Maglev connects Pudong Airport with Longyang Road. It is fast, but it does not go directly into central districts such as People's Square, Jing'an, Xuhui, or the Former French Concession. Most travellers still need to transfer to the metro or take a taxi from Longyang Road. Best for: people staying near Line 2, people travelling light, or visitors who want the Maglev experience.

Airport Link Line

The Airport Link Line is an important option for travel between Pudong Airport, Hongqiao Airport, Hongqiao Railway Station, and other parts of the city. It is especially useful if you need to transfer between airports or connect from Pudong to the Hongqiao transport hub. Best for:

  • Pudong Airport to Hongqiao Airport
  • Pudong Airport to Hongqiao Railway Station
  • Airport-to-airport transfers
  • Travellers going toward western or southern Shanghai
  • People who want rail transport but want to avoid multiple metro transfers

Hongqiao Airport (SHA) to the city

Hongqiao Airport is much closer to central Shanghai than Pudong. Metro Lines 2 and 10 are usually convenient, depending on your destination. Taxis and Didi are also practical because the distance to central Puxi is shorter.

Also useful

Bikes, buses, and ferries

Once you understand the metro and Didi, bikes, buses, and ferries can make getting around Shanghai easier and more interesting.

Bike sharing in Shanghai

Shared bikes are useful for short trips, last-mile transport, and exploring neighbourhoods. They are common around metro stations, residential compounds, shopping streets, office areas, and university zones. Popular bike-sharing options include Meituan Bike, Hello Bike, and other bikes available through local apps or mini programs.

Use shared bikes for:

  • Short trips under 15–20 minutes
  • Getting from a metro station to your final destination
  • Exploring neighbourhoods such as Xuhui, Jing'an, Former French Concession, or riverside areas
  • Avoiding short taxi rides during traffic

Bike-sharing tips:

  • Park only in allowed parking areas.
  • Do not block pavements, gates, ramps, or building entrances.
  • Be careful around scooters and delivery riders.
  • Avoid busy roads until you are comfortable with local traffic.
  • Check the brakes and seat before starting.
  • Do not ride after drinking.
  • In summer, short bike trips can still feel hot and tiring.

Buses in Shanghai

Buses are cheap and cover many areas that the metro does not reach directly. They are useful for local neighbourhood trips, short journeys, and routes where the metro would require several transfers. However, buses are usually less beginner-friendly than the metro because stops may be harder to identify, traffic affects journey time, and some route information is easier to understand in Chinese.

Depending on the route and your setup, you may be able to pay with the Shanghai Public Transportation QR code, Alipay, WeChat, SH MaaS / Suishenxing, the Shanghai Public Transportation Card, or cash on some routes.

Buses are helpful when:

  • Your destination is not close to a metro station.
  • You are travelling within one neighbourhood.
  • You want the cheapest possible option.
  • A map app shows a direct bus route.
  • You are already familiar with the area.

Bus tips for newcomers:

  • Use a map app to follow your route while riding.
  • Check whether you need to scan when boarding only or also when exiting.
  • Keep an eye on stop names.
  • Allow extra time during rush hour.
  • Start with simple direct routes before using complex transfers.

Ferries in Shanghai

Shanghai ferries cross the Huangpu River and can be both practical and scenic. They are different from tourist river cruises. Ferries are short public transport crossings used by pedestrians, cyclists, scooter riders, and commuters.

Use ferries when:

  • You want a cheap Huangpu River crossing.
  • You are exploring the Bund, Lujiazui, or riverside areas.
  • You are travelling with a bike or scooter.
  • You want a local Shanghai experience.
  • You want skyline views without paying for a cruise.

Ferry tips:

  • Check the route and operating hours before going.
  • Some ferry routes are more scenic than others.
  • Ferries can be useful for photography, especially around sunset.
  • Bring your transport QR code or public transportation card.
  • Do not confuse commuter ferries with sightseeing cruises.

Just landed?

First 24 hours in Shanghai: transport setup

If you have just arrived, do this first:

  1. 01Set up Alipay or WeChat Pay

    Do this before you arrive if possible — link a foreign card, complete verification, and test a small payment.

  2. 02Save your address in Chinese

    Keep your hotel, apartment, or school address saved in Chinese to show taxi drivers or paste into Didi.

  3. 03Take an official taxi or Didi from the airport

    The easiest option if you have heavy luggage. Use the official taxi queue, never touts inside the terminal.

  4. 04Use the metro if travelling light

    If you arrive during operating hours, Metro Line 2, the Maglev, or the Airport Link Line can save money.

  5. 05Download SH MaaS / Suishenxing

    One app and one QR code for metro, buses, ferries, and airport rail, plus route planning and last-train times.

  6. 06Save your nearest metro station

    Learn its name in English and Chinese — it becomes your reference point for the whole city.

  7. 07Check the last train time

    Last trains vary by line and station, so check before going out at night.

  8. 08Keep a small amount of cash

    Cash is a backup for some taxis and ticket machines if your phone dies or a QR code fails.

Frequently asked questions

Shanghai transportation questions

What is the best way to get around Shanghai?

For most newcomers, the best combination is metro plus Didi. Use the metro for daily travel and Didi or taxis for late nights, luggage, bad weather, or destinations far from a station.

Is Shanghai public transport easy for foreigners?

Yes, especially the metro. Metro signs usually include English, and mobile payment options make riding easier than before. Buses and ferries are useful too, but they may take more confidence at first.

Can foreigners use the Shanghai Metro?

Yes. Foreigners can use the Shanghai Metro with mobile payment apps, transport QR codes, metro cards, single-journey tickets, and supported contactless bank cards. See the Shanghai Metro guide for details.

Can I use a foreign bank card on Shanghai public transport?

Foreign bank cards are increasingly supported, especially for metro ticket purchases and contactless metro gates. However, it is still best to set up Alipay, WeChat Pay, or SH MaaS as your main transport payment method.

Is Didi available in Shanghai?

Yes. Didi is widely used in Shanghai and is one of the easiest ways for foreigners to book rides without explaining addresses in Chinese. Our taxis and Didi guide covers booking and payment.

Are taxis expensive in Shanghai?

Taxis are more expensive than the metro or bus but are still practical for short trips, luggage, late-night travel, and airport journeys. Always use official taxis and make sure the meter is used.

What is the cheapest way to travel in Shanghai?

The cheapest options are usually the metro and bus. Shared bikes are also affordable for short neighbourhood trips.

What is the best way from Pudong Airport to downtown Shanghai?

The best option depends on your destination. Metro Line 2 is usually the cheapest, the Maglev is fast to Longyang Road, the Airport Link Line is useful for Hongqiao connections, and taxis or Didi are easiest with luggage. Compare them in our Pudong Airport guide.

Is the Shanghai Maglev worth it?

The Maglev is worth it if you want a fast ride from Pudong Airport to Longyang Road or want to experience one of Shanghai's most famous transport options. It may not be the most convenient choice if your final destination is far from Longyang Road.

Can tourists use buses in Shanghai?

Yes. Tourists can use buses, but the metro is usually easier for first-time visitors. Buses are best once you are comfortable using map apps and transport QR codes.

Can I use shared bikes in Shanghai?

Yes. Shared bikes are useful for short trips and last-mile travel, especially around metro stations and residential areas. Park responsibly and avoid busy roads until you are comfortable with local traffic.

Do taxis in Shanghai accept cash?

Some taxis may accept cash, but mobile payment is much more common. Keep cash as a backup, but do not rely on it as your only payment method.
