Living in the Former French Concession, Shanghai.

Tree-lined streets, lane houses, independent cafes, and more character than anywhere else in the city. The FFC is one of Shanghai's most loved places to live.

Overview Metro and transport Housing Trade-offs

Best for

People who want atmosphere as much as convenience

The Former French Concession — known as the FFC — is not a single administrative district but a historic area that spans parts of Xuhui and Huangpu. It is defined by its plane tree canopy, preserved lane houses (shikumen), and dense concentration of independent cafes, restaurants, boutiques, and bars. Expats who choose the FFC typically trade a degree of transport convenience for a neighbourhood that feels genuinely distinctive from any other in Shanghai.

At a glance

What the FFC is like

Location

Central Puxi, straddling Xuhui and Huangpu

The FFC broadly covers the area between Huaihai Middle Road in the north, Xujiahui in the south, Zhongshan South 2nd Road in the east, and Wulumuqi Road in the west.

  • Wukang Road — most photographed street, heritage villas
  • Anfu Road — independent cafes, boutiques, galleries
  • Fuxing Road — wide, leafy, residential
  • Xintiandi and Tianzifang — cultural and dining hubs
Vibe

Bohemian, leafy, and walkable

The FFC is the part of Shanghai that most surprises first-time visitors — canopied streets, quiet lanes, and a density of cafes, brunch spots, and wine bars that feels more like a European city than a Chinese metropolis.

  • Heavy plane tree canopy — especially beautiful in autumn
  • Independent cafes and restaurants on almost every block
  • Weekend street life, art galleries, and boutique shopping
  • Quieter residential lanes just steps from busier streets
Best for

Who chooses the FFC

The FFC attracts expats who value character, walkability, and a strong cafe and dining culture — and are willing to accept slightly less modern housing and less predictable transport.

  • Young professionals and couples without children
  • Creatives, educators, and media professionals
  • Longer-term Shanghai residents who know the city
  • People who work from home or have flexible commutes

Getting around

Metro lines and transport

The FFC has reasonable metro access, though some of the quieter residential lanes are a 10 to 15 minute walk from the nearest station. Didi and cycling fill the gaps well.

  • Line 1 North–south through the FFC

    Changshu Road and Hengshan Road stations are the main FFC stops. Connects south to Xujiahui and north toward People's Square and the city centre.
  • Line 9 East toward the Bund, west toward Songjiang

    Dapuqiao station is useful for reaching the waterfront and the eastern FFC. Passes through Xujiahui and connects to Zhongchun Road in the west.
  • Line 10 Xintiandi and Laoximen connections

    Xintiandi station is at the north-east edge of the FFC. Line 10 connects to Yuyuan Garden, the Old City, and Hongqiao Airport in the west.
  • Line 12 Running along the southern FFC

    Jiashan Road station is useful for southern FFC streets. Line 12 connects to Jing'an Temple in the west and Century Avenue in Pudong in the east.
  • Bike Cycling works exceptionally well here

    The flat streets, low traffic on residential lanes, and short distances between the FFC's key areas make Meituan Bike or Hello Bike one of the best ways to get around locally.

Renting

Housing in the FFC

The FFC offers a wider range of housing character than most Shanghai neighbourhoods — from renovated lane houses to modern apartment towers — but older stock can mean older amenities.

  • Type Lane houses, renovated shikumen, and modern apartments

    Renovated lane houses and shikumen (stone gate houses) are the most sought-after options, but can be harder to find and maintain. Modern apartment buildings are available on the district's main roads.
  • Price Variable — character costs more than convenience

    Well-maintained lane houses command a premium for the atmosphere. Modern apartment blocks in the area are comparable to Jing'an in price. The oldest buildings can be cheaper but may have older plumbing and heating.
  • Check Inspect older buildings carefully before signing

    Heating, hot water, insulation, and elevator access can vary significantly in older lane house conversions. Visit in person during different times of day and ask about building management before committing.

Honest assessment

Trade-offs to consider

  • Pro The best cafe, dining, and street culture in Shanghai

    No other neighbourhood has the same concentration of independent cafes, restaurants, bars, and walkable street life. For many expats, this defines quality of life in Shanghai.
  • Pro Genuinely distinctive character

    The plane tree canopy, lane house architecture, and neighbourhood scale make the FFC feel unlike any other part of Shanghai — or most cities in China.
  • Con Less practical for families with children

    Apartments tend to be smaller, international schools are not as close as in Changning or Jinqiao, and compound facilities (pools, gyms, playgrounds) are much rarer.
  • Con Older housing stock requires more careful inspection

    Lane houses and older buildings can have charm but also maintenance issues. Budget for potential heating, hot water, and insulation shortcomings in winter.
